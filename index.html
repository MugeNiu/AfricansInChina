China's "Little Africa"

Tens of thousands of Africans come to China in search of a better life every year, making them the largest expat group in Guangzhou. They have carved out a space in the city and challenged the homogenous culture along the way.

Hear Their Stories

"I came here because China is the next big thing. You may deny that but it is true. As a student, I just want something really small--the Chinese experience."

Mark moved from his home country Kenya to Guangzhou to pursue a degree in international business. The Chinese government promised to give 18,000 scholarships to students like Mark between 2013 and 2015, many of whom are studying business-related subjects. Obtaining the Chinese work visa is a long and arduous process for foreigners and many internaitonal students are forced to leave or overstay their visas after graduation.

"I am stranded here, really stranded...I have nowhere to sleep and nowhere to go."

After losing his parents to the war in Sierra Leone, Eric King came to China in hopes of furthering his education and making a living. But since his tourism visa expired a year after his arrival, King found himself struggling to avoid police inspection, find a stable job and even a place to stay at night.

Eddie moved from a village in Guinea seven years ago. Now he owns a clothing store in the commercial district in Guangzhou. Married to a Chinese woman with one child, Eddie says he considers China his own country and is thankful for the business opportunities and security it offers. However, the chance of getting Chinese citizenship is still slim for people like Eddie.

"The Chinese are very business-friendly. Living in China is not difficult, provided you are a law-abiding citizen in your own country. In China they tell you, 'don't do drug business,' well it's the same in my country, same in America."

Three times a year, Sam Bimba comes to buy clothing in Guangzhou to sell back to Liberia. On his business trips, he likes to spend time in the neighborhood of Xiaobeilu, where an active African business community gathers. Each time he can only spend up to 30 days in the country before his visa expires, but Sam said he understands foreigners need to respect the laws and regulations of the land.
